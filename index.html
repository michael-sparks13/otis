A "Nightmare Scenario" in the Pacific

Hurricane Otis went from relatively mild storm to the strongest hurricane to ever hit southwestern Mexico in under 24 hours

A "Nightmare Scenario" in the Pacific

Hurricane Otis went from relatively mild storm to the strongest hurricane to ever hit southwestern Mexico in under 24 hours

written and created by Michael Sparks

On Saturday, October 14, 2023, meteorologists at the National Hurricane Center in Miami put out an innocuous seven-day outlook: several hundred miles south of Mexico, a low-pressure system, which plays a crucial role in storm development, was beginning to form in the Pacific Ocean. A tropical depression, the outlook said, “is expected to form during the latter part of next week while the system meanders well offshore of the coast of Mexico.”

That tropical depression did form. But what was expected to meander well offshore eventually rammed directly into Acapulco, as the strongest and, ultimately, most expensive storm to ever hit Mexico’s western coast.

The National Hurricane Center typically puts out public advisories once storms coalesce into tropical depressions. These notices typically contain a five-day forecast within a “cone of uncertainty.” The NHC put out its first advisory for Tropical Depression Eighteen-E at 10 am Acapulco time on Sunday the 22nd.

Six hours later, the now tropical storm is given the name Otis.

It isn’t until 24 hours after the first advisory that Acapulco is at the center of the forecasted path. It was, by then, mid-morning on Monday the 23rd.

1 pm Tuesday the 24th: the NHC publishes advisory 9A, officially upgrading the storm to Hurricane Otis.

Just tweleve hours later, at 1am Wednesday morning, Otis made landfall.
